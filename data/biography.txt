# Doctoral Dissertation
Lamonte's dissertation title is Designing a Resilience-Oriented Cyber-Secure Machine Learning Framework for Operational Technology in Autonomous Vehicle Systems Enhanced by Advanced Telecommunications Infrastructure.
The dissertation addresses a foundational gap in AV cybersecurity research — the exclusion of the operational technology (OT) layer from system-level security models.
The core research problem is that prior AV cybersecurity research has overemphasized IT, data privacy, and perception systems while neglecting the OT layer where control systems, safety actuators, and real-time ML decision-making intersect.
The dissertation proposes and validates a machine learning-based cybersecurity architecture designed specifically for the OT layer of autonomous vehicle systems.
The architecture detects and recovers from adversarial attacks at the control system level, with architectural awareness of telecommunications infrastructure, latency thresholds, and safety-critical timing constraints.
The study uses a convergent mixed-methods design combining quantitative simulation of adversarial scenarios with qualitative insight from subject matter experts.
Simulated adversarial attacks use Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) and Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) across multiple attack intensities.
The simulation environment includes tools such as CARLA, ROS, Gazebo, SUMO, and OMNeT++ for network emulation.
The qualitative component engages six to ten domain experts in cybersecurity, AV systems, and intelligent transportation infrastructure.
The dissertation is grounded in a hybrid theoretical framework combining Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) Security Theory, Adversarial Machine Learning Theory, and Resilience Engineering Theory.
The study synthesizes over 120 peer-reviewed APA 7 sources from 2020 to 2025.
The dissertation identifies five key gaps in prior research — lack of adversarial ML defenses in OT-specific autonomous systems, no empirical evaluation under real-time latency constraints, minimal integration with telecommunications attack surfaces like 5G and V2X, absence of unified resilience frameworks, and the fact that existing solutions are IT-centric rather than OT-driven.
The research positions Lamonte's work as the first advanced cybersecurity architecture grounded in operational technology for autonomous vehicle systems.
The dissertation follows Walsh College's doctoral template and APA 7 formatting standards throughout.
Lamonte's doctoral writing follows strict standards — third-person active voice, no hedge phrases, no passive constructions, em-dashes for inline clarification, and APA 7 citations mid-sentence or at sentence end.
